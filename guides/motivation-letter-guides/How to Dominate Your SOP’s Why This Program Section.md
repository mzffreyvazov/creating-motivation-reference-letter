# How to Dominate Your SOP’s "Why This Program" Section
**By Jordan Dotson**  
*Updated: August 13, 2024*

When it comes to the graduate statement of purpose, most applicants find it difficult to explain why a particular university is the right place for them. Many don’t even attempt this at all. They just describe their background, hope for the best, then cry into their boba when rejections pile up.

Others flatter the school in a short paragraph at the end—a classic “Kiss of Death.” Others copy-paste brochure cliches from the department website, parroting what the university has already told them they “will learn.”

All are a shame, because your **SOP Why This Program section** is, in my opinion, the single most important part of your application.

The SOP is the only way for grad schools to tell if you’ve got a realistic plan. If you know what you’re doing. If you have legitimate reasons for applying to their uber-competitive school, or if you’re just another desperate kid with a vague belief that a grad degree will make you employable. As I often say, this is why it’s called a statement of **PURPOSE**—it’s about your plans for the future.

Writing a brilliant “Why This Program” section isn’t difficult. It just takes a little thought and reverse-engineering.

---

## 1. “Why This Program?” Doesn’t Mean “Why Graduate School?”

Grad schools don’t care why you’re applying for a master’s or PhD in general. In fact, they already know. It’s the reason for their very existence.

*   **MSCS programs** exist to help computer science students gain expertise in certain subfields.
*   **PhD programs in Public Policy** exist to train researchers who can inform governments.

When they read your SOP, they are looking for something else entirely: **they want to know why you’re applying to them specifically.**

*   If you’re applying for an Ed.M. in Counseling Psychology at **Columbia**, why do you prefer them over the awesome programs at Penn and Northwestern? What’s unique about Columbia?
*   If you’re applying to the M.S. in Nuclear Engineering at **Georgia Tech**, how are you certain they’ll help you achieve your specific career goals?

## 2. “Why This Program?” Also Doesn’t Mean “What Will You Learn?”

The most common mistake students make here is to paraphrase generalized lingo from the website, describing what they “will learn” from the program.

**The Mistake:**
> “At Columbia Teacher’s College, I will obtain first-rate practitioner preparation in the context of first-rate scholarship. I will learn to apply these skills in clinical practice, administration, assessment, and research...”

Believe me. They know you will learn these skills. That’s why they created the program. This doesn’t tell them anything about **YOU**. Even worse, this statement doesn’t differentiate Columbia from any other school.

**WARNING:** If you’ve written *“In this program, I will learn…”* in your "Why This Program" section, there’s a strong chance you’re telling the school what they already know. This is a “Thank you Captain Obvious!” moment.

It’s **NOT** about what the school offers you. It’s about what **YOU** bring to the table.

---

## Step 1: Figure Out Your Ultimate Career Goal

You need a "Sentence of Purpose." This is a thesis statement that explains what you hope to achieve in the future in holistic terms.

**Example:**
> “This is why I apply to Gotham University today: to learn to disentangle data, construct valid inferences, and design clinical trials which apply statistical methods for improved cancer-screening tests and treatments.”

You also need a smart “Career Goals Statement.” This tells the admissions committee exactly which specific career outcome you’re hoping to achieve.

**Example:**
> “After graduation, I hope to transition into a Senior Data Scientist role in companies like WayneHealth or Stark Medical, ideally working on projects that implement data-driven recommendations for automated batch processes and servers.”

### **DO THIS!**
1.  **Visualize your dream career.** Where is it? Which city? What’s the exact job title?
2.  **Find listings.** Visit Indeed or ZipRecruiter and find listings for jobs you’d love to have. Find AT LEAST two companies you’d like to work for.
3.  **For PhD Applicants:** Decide whether you want to work in industry or academia. Even if you want to be a professor, contemplate what your dream research looks like in the future. Articulate your professional dream.

## Step 2: Reverse Engineer the Skills You Need to Achieve Your Goal

Let’s say you want to get hired as a Mechanical Engineer for a giant multinational company like Siemens. Your dream job description lists necessary skills: *Solidworks, FEA, finite element analysis, etc.*

These are the skills you need to master to be qualified for your dream job. You probably already have experience with a few, but know you’re lacking in others.

**That means you have a VALID reason for applying to grad school—to fill those gaps!**

### **DO THIS!**
1.  **List the skills.** Identify what you need to qualify for your dream job but don’t yet possess.
2.  **For Thesis/PhD Applicants:** Figure out the lab skills you need to acquire. Get as specific as possible about the topics you want to research.
3.  **For "Clueless" PhD Applicants:** Figure out which skills and perspectives will be most helpful in exploring your dream research topics. Econometrics? Computational frameworks? Figure out the angles that seem the most intellectually compelling to you.

## Step 3: Explain How Your Target University Will Give You Those Skills

Classes. Professors with strange expertise. Capstone projects. Wildly unique labs. Co-op programs. Corporate sponsorships.

As you browse your target schools’ websites, look for the "News & Events," "Faculty," and "Industry Partners" sections. Each of these items represents a resource you can use.

You’ve visualized your career goal. You’ve visualized the skills you need. **Now, visualize how you’re going to obtain those skills at your target university.**

### **DO THIS!**
*   **Match skills to resources.** For every skill or niche expertise you listed in Step 2, find a way to obtain it at your target school.
    *   Do they have an entire class dedicated to the Mocha testing framework?
    *   Will they place you in a semester-long co-op as a school counselor?
    *   Do they have a professor whose research dovetails with your interest?
*   **PhD Applicants:** This will revolve entirely around finding suitable professors.
*   **Master's Applicants:** This will be a combination of specific coursework, internships, and labs.

## Step 4: Don’t Forget to Answer the Question “Why?”

As you write your "Why This Program" section, don’t just “list” everything that looks cool. Instead, tell them **why** it’s so important for you.

**Bad / Average:**
> “At Gotham University, the Modern Unit Testing Frameworks course will give me a solid foundation in both Mocha and Chai.”

**Better:**
> “At Gotham University, the Modern Unit Testing Frameworks course will give me a solid foundation in both Mocha and Chai, **skills necessary for a software engineer who hopes to work in on-demand cloud computing platforms like Azure or AWS.**”

By connecting the skill to your ultimate goal, you show the school that you really understand how to make full use of their resources.

**Bad / Average:**
> “I am interested in working with Professor Charles Xavier because his research in Mutant Cognitive Behavioral Therapy aligns with my own.”

**Better:**
> “I am interested in working with Professor Charles Xavier because his current research in Mutant Cognitive Behavioral Therapy **aligns with my goals in Clinical Telepathy.**”

**Awesome:**
> “I am interested in working with Professor Charles Xavier because his current research in Mutant Cognitive Behavioral Therapy aligns with my goals in Clinical Telepathy. **Specifically, in his recent study of high-functioning autism occurrence in telepaths, he employed the novel Grey Jumpstart technique which I believe will be integral in my own research on Psionic Shields.**”

Whatever you highlight about the school, make sure it shows clearly how it fills some gap in your current expertise, and how it will springboard you toward the career of your dreams.

---

## Conclusion

When evaluating applicants, grad programs look for the students who will make the best use of their resources. They don’t want the student who shows up with their hands out, mumbling, *"Give me knowledge, please sir."*

They want the fierce, smiling students who say:

> “I’m going to take **THESE** exact classes, and work in **THIS** exact lab, and conduct research with **THIS** professor, and then immediately apply for **THIS** kind of job working on **THIS** kind of project in **THESE** kinds of companies, and **THAT** is why I need **YOU**, Stanford. Because I can’t achieve my dreams without **YOU**.”

To dominate your "Why This Program" section:

1.  **Visualize** your dream career.
2.  **Reverse engineer** the skills you’ll need to be qualified.
3.  **Find specific ways** to obtain those skills in your target school.
4.  **Say explicitly** that gaining these skills, at this school, will be necessary for you to achieve your goals.
