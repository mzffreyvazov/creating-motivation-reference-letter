## 1. What They Are Likely Evaluating

According to the official Turkiye Scholarships evaluation process, applications are judged on academic success, social activities, and motivation related to the chosen field. The expert evaluation also considers academic interests, career goals, consistency of preferences, the content of the letter of intent, and participation in social activities. The interview usually lasts 15-30 minutes and covers:

- Document check
- Purpose of application
- Academic knowledge and career goals
- Candidate questions to the committee

For you, this means they will probably not try to deeply test transformer mathematics. They will more likely ask whether your story is coherent:

- Why Turkiye?
- Why artificial intelligence and data engineering?
- Why Ankara University?
- Why this research topic?
- Why should they believe you will return and benefit Azerbaijan?
- Are you technically ready for graduate study?
- Are you mature enough to represent the scholarship well?

Your best interview posture: calm, specific, and useful. Avoid sounding like you want the scholarship only as an escape route. Sound like someone who has already found a real problem and needs the right environment to solve it.

Practical note: bring every document and certificate connected to your latest education and application. The official page says candidates invited to interview must bring their documents. Also check your invitation carefully: the official process mentions tests before interviews in some cases, and it has a separate note for candidates from Azerbaijan. If your invitation includes an exam slot, review basic mathematics, geometry, and logic, but keep your main preparation focused on your motivation letter and research proposal.

## 8. Your Research Proposal in Simple Language

Question: "Explain your research proposal."

Answer:

> My research proposal is about building an open-source Azerbaijani embedding model for RAG systems. In a RAG system, the model first retrieves relevant documents, then the language model generates an answer based on those documents. If retrieval is weak, the final answer becomes unreliable even if the LLM is powerful. Today, most embedding models are trained mainly on high-resource languages, so they often do not perform well on Azerbaijani, especially in legal or administrative texts. My plan is to collect and clean an Azerbaijani corpus, adapt a multilingual model such as XLM-R to Azerbaijani data, fine-tune it for sentence similarity and retrieval, then evaluate it against models like mBERT and LaBSE using metrics such as top-k accuracy, MRR, and NDCG. The important part is that the model, code, and benchmark would be released openly.

If they look non-technical, simplify:

> In simple terms, I want Azerbaijani search systems to understand meaning, not only keywords. If someone asks a legal question in Azerbaijani, the system should retrieve the right legal passages even when the wording is different.

## 9. Technical Questions They Might Ask

### What is RAG?

> RAG means Retrieval-Augmented Generation. It combines information retrieval with text generation. First, the system searches a document collection and retrieves relevant passages. Then a language model generates an answer using those passages. This improves factuality because the model is grounded in external documents instead of relying only on its internal memory.

### What is an embedding model?

> An embedding model converts text into numerical vectors. Texts with similar meaning should have vectors close to each other. In search systems, embeddings make it possible to retrieve documents by semantic meaning, not only exact keywords.

### Why not just use existing multilingual models?

> Existing multilingual models are useful baselines, but they are usually trained with far more data from languages like English than Azerbaijani. Azerbaijani is agglutinative and low-resource, so general multilingual models may miss morphology, domain terminology, and local usage. Domain adaptation can improve performance for Azerbaijani legal and administrative text.

### Why XLM-R?

> XLM-R is a strong open-source multilingual transformer trained on many languages. Starting from it is practical because it already has cross-lingual knowledge, and then I can adapt it further to Azerbaijani instead of training a model from zero.

### What is contrastive learning?

> Contrastive learning trains the model to bring semantically similar text pairs closer together in vector space and push unrelated pairs apart. For retrieval, this is useful because the model learns what should be considered a good match for a query.

### What are MRR and NDCG?

> MRR, mean reciprocal rank, measures how high the first correct result appears in the retrieval list. NDCG evaluates the ranking quality when multiple results can be relevant and relevance can have different levels. Both help measure whether the search system returns useful results near the top.

### What is the biggest risk in your proposal?

> The biggest risk is creating high-quality evaluation data. Training data can be collected from public sources, but evaluation requires reliable query-document pairs. To manage this, I would start with a smaller carefully validated benchmark, use structured legal documents to generate candidate pairs, and include native-speaker validation. I would rather have a smaller trustworthy benchmark than a larger noisy one.

### Is this too ambitious for a master's thesis?

> The full vision is broad, but the master's thesis can be scoped carefully. The minimum successful output would be a cleaned corpus, a baseline comparison, one adapted open-source embedding model, and a small but reliable retrieval benchmark. Further model scaling or expansion to other Turkic languages can come after the thesis.
